About the Workshop

Technology drives discovery in life sciences and in particular microscopy had a strong impact on progress in understanding biological systems across scales. Light sheet microscopy has emerged in the last 15 years as a powerful tool to interrogate biological systems. Its unique properties enable unprecedented imaging speeds that due to reduced phototoxicity enable monitoring of living systems for long periods of time. Large, fixed or cleared biological samples, such as organs or embryos, can be imaged with high resolution in a fraction of time compared to other light microscopy approaches. The spatial coverage and temporal resolution, when combined, leads to in toto imaging of entire biological systems. Light sheets open entirely new windows into the inner workings of cells and organisms and has re-invigorated several research fields including developmental and cell biology, physiology, neurobiology and biodiversity research. In this workshop, which is a 10th Anniversary instalment of the Light Sheet Fluorescence Microscopy (LSFM) workshop/conference that started in 2009 in Dresden, we plan to bring together the technology developers and users of light sheets. It will be an opportunity to showcase the latest light sheet set-ups and discuss how to best deploy them for the benefit of the biological research community.

Selection Critiria

The course will be open to altogether 16 students.

We are aiming to establish four interdisciplinary student teams consisting of 2 biologists, 1 computer scientist and 1 physicist/microscopist each. After the initial intense introduction to light sheet technology, the teams will tackle research problems defined in their applications. The applicants are therefore encouraged to write a short, one page motivation letter, where they:

  • describe their educational background.
  • summarize their skill set in biology, computer science and microscopy.
  • and ideally propose a concise imaging experiment that could benefit from light sheet microscopy.

Please note that MPI-CBG is equipped to deal with most major model organisms and we encourage also biologists working on emerging models to apply.

Additionally the applicants should submit their CV, publication record and summary of online presence (websites, blogs, Figshare, wiki pages, social media handles, github etc.).

Transportation

Via Plane

Dresden has a relatively small airport connected by Lufthansa to major German hubs such as Frankfurt, Munich and Düesseldorf. There is also an AirFrance flight from London.

Berlin Schoenefeld International airport is about 172 km from Dresden, easily reachable by regular trains.

Vaclav Havel Airport Prague in the Czech Republic is another option, 160 km away, also connected by trains about every two hours.

From the Dresden Airport, please take the train S2 available every 30 min to the train station Bahnhof-Neustadt. Then take the tram 6, in the direction Niedersedlitz, to the station Königsheimplatz. Cross the street and walk along the street Schubertstrasse till you come to Pfotenhauerstrasse and turn left. Our institute is at Pfotenhauerstrasse 108. It is a green and blue building. It takes about approx. 40 minutes.
